LAG

In poker terminology, LAG stands for "Loose Aggressive" - a style of play characterized by playing a wider range of hands more aggressively than the average player. A LAG player tends to play many hands and isn't afraid to bet and raise frequently, often putting their opponents under pressure.

What is a LAG?

In the poker world, it's crucial to understand different player types and strategies. One such type is the Loose Aggressive or LAG player. This is a player who participates in a lot of hands (loose) and tends to bet and raise frequently (aggressive).

A LAG strategy can be very effective in both cash games and tournaments, across all poker variants. The aggressive betting style can often force opponents to make mistakes, while the wide range of hands can make a LAG player unpredictable and tough to play against.

However, the LAG style also carries risks. It requires a deep understanding of poker strategy and a good read on opponents. Without these, a LAG player can end up losing their chips quickly.

Example of a LAG

Let's say you're playing a game of Texas Hold'em. A player to your left has been involved in almost every hand, constantly raising and re-raising. They aren't waiting for premium hands; they're betting with a wide range of cards. This player is implementing a LAG strategy.

  • "She's really pushing the action, such a LAG player."
  • "He's playing LAG tonight, getting involved in every hand."
  • "You're playing like a LAG, constantly raising and betting."

Late Position

Late Position refers to the last few positions to act in a round of betting, usually the 'button' and the 'cut-off'. These are advantageous positions as these players get to see how all the other players act before they have to make their own decision.

What is Late Position?

In poker, the Late Position refers to the last few positions to act in a round of betting. The specific positions considered 'late' can vary, but they typically include the 'button' and the 'cut-off'. The 'button' is the last player to act in a round of betting, and the 'cut-off' is the player to the right of the 'button'.

The Advantage of Late Position

Being in Late Position is generally considered an advantage in poker. Players in Late Position have the opportunity to see how all the other players act before they make their decision. This additional information can be used to adjust their strategy accordingly. For instance, if all the players before them fold, a player in Late Position might decide to play a wider range of hands.

How to Play from Late Position

When playing from Late Position, it's generally recommended to play a wider range of hands. This is because there are fewer players left to act after you, which reduces the likelihood that another player has a strong hand. Also, if you're in Late Position and everyone else has checked, you have the opportunity to 'steal' the pot with a well-timed bet, regardless of your actual hand strength.

Example of "Late Position"

For example, if you're in Late Position and everyone before you has folded, you might choose to bet or raise, regardless of your hand, in an attempt to steal the blinds.

  • "From Late Position, she was able to steal the blinds after everyone else folded."
  • "Being in Late Position allowed him to play a wider range of hands."
  • "He used his Late Position to gather information and adjust his betting strategy."

Lead

The term 'Lead' in poker refers to the act of being the first player to make a bet into the pot on any given round. It's a strategy often employed when a player has a strong hand or wants to bluff their opponents.

What is a Lead in Poker?

In poker, to 'Lead' means to be the first player to make a bet into the pot on any given betting round. This action is often taken when a player has a strong hand, or sometimes as a bluff to make opponents think they have a stronger hand than they actually do.

Understanding the Lead

Understanding when to lead can be crucial in poker. Leading can show strength and put pressure on your opponents, but it can also expose you to raises from players with stronger hands. Leading is generally more common in post-flop betting rounds.

Using the Lead

Using the lead effectively requires a good understanding of your hand strength, your opponents' tendencies, and the overall game context. Leading with weak hands as a bluff can be risky, but can also pay off if your opponents are cautious or have weak hands themselves.

Example of "Lead"

For instance, if you're playing a hand of Texas Hold'em and the flop comes out, being the first player to bet would be taking the lead.

  • "After the flop, he decided to take the lead and bet, putting pressure on his opponents."
  • "She often took the lead in betting, using aggressive tactics to intimidate her opponents."
  • "Despite having a weak hand, he decided to lead with a large bet as a bluff."

Leak

A Leak in poker refers to a consistent strategic mistake that a player makes, which reduces their overall profitability in the game. These can be identified and corrected to improve the player's performance.

What is a Leak?

In poker, a Leak is a consistent strategic mistake or bad habit that a player makes during a game. These mistakes, while they might seem minor, can add up over time and significantly reduce a player's winnings or increase their losses. Leaks can occur in various aspects of a player's strategy, including their betting, bluffing, or hand selection strategies.

Identifying and Correcting Leaks

Identifying and correcting Leaks is a key part of improving as a poker player. This often involves reviewing and analysing your play to spot any consistent mistakes or suboptimal strategies. Once a Leak has been identified, the next step is to adjust your strategy to correct it. This could involve studying poker theory, getting coaching, or simply practicing more mindful and disciplined play.

Common Types of Leaks

There are many types of Leaks that players can have in poker. Some players might consistently play too many hands, leading to a hand selection Leak. Others might bluff too often or rarely at all, creating a bluffing Leak. Still others might not pay enough attention to their opponents' actions, resulting in a positional awareness Leak.

Example of "Leak"

For instance, if you consistently call bets with weak hands hoping to hit a lucky card, this would be considered a Leak. Over time, this strategy is likely to result in more losses than wins.

  • "He identified a Leak in his play - he was bluffing too often and his opponents were catching on."
  • "Her biggest Leak was playing too many weak hands out of position."
  • "After reviewing his games, he realised his Leak was not paying enough attention to his opponents' betting patterns."

Level

A 'Level' in poker refers to a specific period in a tournament during which the blinds and/or antes stay the same. Each level has a set duration, after which the blinds and/or antes increase.

What is a Level in Poker?

In poker, a 'Level' refers to a period during a tournament where the blinds and/or antes remain the same. Each level lasts for a set duration (usually ranging from 15 minutes to an hour), and when that time expires, the blinds and/or antes increase, and the tournament moves on to the next level.

Understanding Levels

Understanding the structure of levels is crucial for tournament poker strategy. As levels progress and blinds and antes increase, players are forced to make more aggressive moves to avoid being 'blinded out' or losing all their chips to the compulsory bets.

Playing Different Levels

The strategy for playing each level can vary. In early levels, players often play conservatively, trying to accumulate chips slowly and avoid early elimination. In later levels, as blinds and antes increase, players may adopt a more aggressive strategy.

Example of "Level"

For instance, in a tournament, Level 1 might have blinds at 25/50, Level 2 at 50/100, Level 3 at 75/150, and so on.

  • "He played conservatively in the early levels of the tournament, but became more aggressive as the blinds increased."
  • "She managed her chip stack well, allowing her to navigate through the levels and make it to the final table."
  • "The levels in this tournament are 30 minutes long, so the blinds increase fairly quickly."

Limp

A Limp in poker refers to when a player only calls the big blind instead of raising. It's typically seen as a passive move and can be indicative of a weak hand, although it can also be used as a strategic play.

What is a Limp?

In poker, to Limp means to call the big blind pre-flop, instead of raising or folding. This is typically seen as a passive play and can indicate a player has a weak or mediocre hand. However, Limping can also be used as a strategic move in certain situations, such as when a player wants to see a cheap flop or set a trap for their opponents.

The Role of Limping in Poker

While Limping is often associated with weak or inexperienced play, it can have a place in a balanced poker strategy. For instance, Limping can be an effective strategy in the right circumstances, such as when you're in late position and several players have already Limped in, or when you have a speculative hand that could become very strong if the right community cards come up.

Limping Strategies and Considerations

When considering whether to Limp, it's important to think about how it will affect the dynamics of the hand and how your opponents might react. If you're Limping with a weak hand, you could be putting yourself at a disadvantage by encouraging more players to enter the pot. On the other hand, Limping with a strong hand could induce your opponents to underestimate your hand strength and make mistakes.

Example of "Limp"

For example, if you're in late position and several players have already Limped in, you might also choose to Limp with a speculative hand like 5-6 suited, hoping to see a cheap flop and hit a strong hand.

  • "He decided to Limp in from early position with his pocket eights, hoping to see a cheap flop."
  • "After several players Limped in ahead of her, she decided to raise and try to take down the pot pre-flop."
  • "Despite having a strong hand, he chose to Limp in, hoping to set a trap for his opponents."

Live Card(s)

A term often used in stud games referring to cards that will improve one's hand that have not yet appeared among other players' up cards, thereby increasing the chance they are still in the deck and thus remain "live."

The term is also used in hold'em to refer to a player having hole cards that no other player has, thus similarly improving the chances of the community cards including cards that make that player a pair. For example, and player is all in with T9 versus an opponent holding AK. While the all-in player is an underdog, both the ten and nine are "live cards" in that if he makes a pair and the player with ace-king doesn't improve, he'll win.
