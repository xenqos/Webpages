Raise

A 'Raise' in poker refers to increasing the current amount of the bet in a betting round.

What is a Raise in Poker?

In poker, a 'Raise' is a move where a player increases the current amount of the bet in a betting round. By raising, a player requires any other player who wants to remain in the hand to at least match their raise, adding more money to the pot.

Understanding Raises

Raising can be a powerful move in poker, used to accomplish various goals. A player may raise to try to force other players to fold, to gain more information about other players' hands, or to increase the size of the pot when they have a strong hand.

Making a Raise

When making a raise, a player must at least double the amount of the previous bet or raise. For example, if the previous bet was $5, a player must raise to at least $10.

Example of "Raise"

For instance, if a player bets $5 and another player raises to $15, the second player has made a raise.

  • "He decided to raise pre-flop with his pair of aces."
  • "She made a raise on the river to put pressure on her opponents."
  • "Despite having a weak hand, he decided to raise as a bluff."

 

Raise First In (RFI)

When a player is first in and enters the pot by raising. Also referred to as open raising.

 

Raise Blind

1) To raise without looking at one's cards.
2) A forced raise.

Rake Explained - How Do Casinos Make Money on Poker?

Casinos make money from poker by taking what's called a rake. The rake in a poker cash game is when the house takes a percentage of the pot. In poker tournaments, a specified portion of the buy-in will go to the house while the rest goes into the prize pool.

Rake Definition - What is Rake in Poker?

Rake in poker is the house's compensation for hosting a poker game. However, not all cardrooms take a rake. In some poker clubs, members are required to pay a membership fee to join the club and then require payment for time spent at the table.

Generally, the rake is explained as the money taken by the casino/cardroom/poker club.

How is Rake Calculated in Poker?

How the rake is calculated varies from game to game and casino to casino. Usually, a poker room will have a set percentage of the pot from each hand go to the house. Generally, anywhere from 5% to 10% of the pot in each poker will be taken for the rake.

The rake will also often be capped. The cap is the maximum amount of rake a poker room can take from a hand.

If you ask a dealer or the floor what the rake is, they will often reply, "the rake is X% capped at $X."

How is Rake Taken in Poker?

The table dealer calculates the rake in live poker games and takes the house fee from the pot. The dealer will work out the pot size and rake accordingly to the percentage set by the poker room.

The rake is automatically taken through the poker software in online poker games.

How is Rake Taken in Poker Tournaments?

In poker tournaments, the rake is paid through your buy-in, often called an entry fee. There may be another percentage from the buy-in taken for the tournament dealers and staff.

For example, in the $10,000 WSOP Main Event, not all of the buy-in goes to the prize pool. For the 2023 WSOP Main Event, 4.9% of the buy-in is withheld for entry fees, and a further 2.1% is taken for the dealers and staff.

How is Rake Taken in Poker Cash Games?

As mentioned, the dealer takes the rake from each pot, but did you know that not every pot has a rake taken?

If a player wins the pot preflop, the house will not take a rake in some poker rooms. For example, a player could raise $10, and a second player could three-bet to $60. A third player could then move all in for $300. If the remaining players folded, the all-in player would make $70 without paying any rake.

However, if a player raises to $10 and gets one caller, and the initial raiser wins the pot on the flop, the house will take a rake as a flop has been dealt.

The above is a general and most common example, but this can vary from poker room to poker room.

How Do Casinos Make Money From Poker?

Casinos, cardrooms and poker rooms make money from poker by taking a rake, entry fee or timed fee from the players.

In poker cash games, the casino often takes a rake from every poker hand in the room. If a poker room hosts 30 cash games, the casino can profit substantially from this. The usual poker hand lasts 1-2 minutes. If that's 30 hands an hour being raked across 30 tables, the house can make a lot of money.

As mentioned with poker tournaments, the rake is included in the buy-in. If a poker tournament has a $100 buy-in, shown as $85+$15, the $85 is allocated for the prize money, while the remaining $15 will be taken as entry fees and staff payments.

Online Poker Rake vs Live Poker Rake - What’s the Difference?

The rake in live games is generally higher than for online poker. Some cardrooms will not take a percentage rake in any community card poker game like Texas Hold'em when a hand does not have a flop. This is called "no flop, no drop".

How to Pay Rake in Live Poker

You don't have to do anything to pay for the rake in live poker, as the table dealer will take the rake from the pot. In raked games, you only pay rake in hands that you play in for live poker.

In timed games, the rake you pay is a set fee. For example, for every 30 minutes, you're required to pay $10. If you play for five hours, you must pay the casino $100.

Can You Beat the Rake in Poker?

In regulated poker games, it is possible to beat the rake. If it weren't possible, there wouldn't be the thousands of professional poker players that exist today. However, in some "underground" poker games, the rake may be a lot higher, which may not be beatable.

You can beat the rake, but unfortunately, it is not something you can avoid. You want to find the games with the lowest rake and cap to have the highest win rate possible.

How to Beat the Rake in Poker

Avoid Small Pots

The rake diminishes, as a percentage of the pot, as the pot grows beyond the size necessary for the maximum rake. For example, in a room with a 10%, $6 maximum rake taken in dollar increments, any pot of $10 on up to $60 will be raked the full 10%. However, a pot over $60 will be raked at less than 10%. And the larger the pot is beyond that, the lower the percentage of the pot will be raked.

Avoid Short-Stacked Games

In an unraked game, it may be advantageous for you to sit in a game with opponents who are short-stacked, depending on how they play and whether you can exploit that play. For example, suppose the short stacks are extremely passive and timid. In that case, you can often have an excellent session by being aggressive in the right spots, regardless of your hand, and getting them to release their hands, giving you small but consistent wins.

But this strategy is rarely effective in a heavily raked game since the huge drain of the large rake on those small pots will often diminish to zero any profit you can make. Even if you are effective with your strategy, it will be offset by a large rake.

Steal the Blinds

Most rooms have the "no flop, no drop" rule, meaning that if there is no flop, there is no rake no matter how large the pot size. Check to make sure. If this is the case, then a strategy of trying to steal the blinds may make sense those hands produce the only small pots that aren't raked, meaning all of the profit goes to you, not to the house.

What is a Range in Poker?

In poker, a 'Range' refers to the set of all hands a player could potentially have in a specific situation. This concept is crucial for strategic decision-making in poker, as it involves estimating the range of hands an opponent might have and adjusting your play accordingly.

Understanding Ranges

The concept of range is fundamental to poker strategy. It involves not just considering an opponent's current hand, but all possible hands they could have based on their actions. Players often use their understanding of ranges to make educated guesses about an opponent's hand and to inform their betting strategy.

Using Range in Poker Strategy

Understanding and using ranges effectively can provide a significant edge in poker. By estimating the range of hands an opponent could have, players can make more informed decisions about when to fold, call, or raise.

Example of "Range"

For instance, if a player always raises with high pairs and high suited connectors, then that is their range in that situation.

  • "He put his opponent on a range of high pairs, influencing his decision to fold."
  • "She narrowed down her opponent's range based on their aggressive betting and decided to call."
  • "Understanding the importance of range, he adjusted his strategy based on his estimate of his opponent's possible hands."

What is a Read in Poker?

In poker, a 'Read' refers to the ability to interpret an opponent's actions, behaviors, or mannerisms during a game, which can provide clues about the strength or weakness of their hand. This is a crucial skill in poker, as it can help inform a player's decisions about whether to fold, call, or raise.

Understanding Reads

Understanding reads requires a good deal of observation, experience, and intuition. It's important to not just focus on the cards, but also to pay attention to your opponents' behavior. Reads can come from physical tells, betting patterns, player conversation, or even previous hands.

Using Reads

Using reads effectively can give players a significant edge in poker. However, it's important to remember that experienced players may use false tells to mislead their opponents, so reads should be used as part of a larger overall strategy, not as the sole basis for decision-making.

Example of "Read"

For instance, if an opponent always scratches their nose when they have a weak hand, this can be a read that can help inform your betting decisions.

  • "He made a successful read on his opponent's bluff, allowing him to win the pot."

Reverse Implied Odds

The opposite of implied odds which estimate what additional chips you will win should you complete a draw or make a better hand than your opponent, reverse implied odds estimate how much you will lose should you complete your draw yet make a hand that is worse than your opponent's. For example, if you are drawing to a low flush and hit your hand yet find yourself up against an opponent drawing to a better flush, reverse implied odds refer to how much you'll lose should the flush be completed.

While most tells are involuntary, a reverse tell is more often done consciously. Acting strong while weak is a common tell; therefore, acting strong when strong can work as a reverse tell. Sometimes called a “false tell.”

River

The 'River' in poker refers to the last community card dealt in certain poker games like Texas Hold'em and Omaha. It's the final opportunity for players to improve their hands or bluff their opponents.

What is a River in Poker?

In poker, the 'River' refers to the fifth and final community card dealt in certain poker games like Texas Hold'em and Omaha. The River is the last card to be dealt, offering players their final opportunity to improve their hand or bluff their opponents.

Understanding the River

The River is a critical stage in poker as it's the last chance for players to decide their betting strategy based on the strength of their final hand. After the River card is dealt, the final round of betting takes place, followed by the showdown where players reveal their hands.

Playing the River

When the River card is dealt, players need to evaluate their hand strength and the potential hands of their opponents. This is also a crucial time for bluffing or making big bets if you have a strong hand.

Example of "River"

For instance, if you're playing a hand of Texas Hold'em and the community cards are A-K-Q-J-10, the 10 would be the River card.

  • "Despite having a weak hand, the River card gave him a flush, winning him the pot."
  • "She decided to make a large bet after the River card was dealt, successfully bluffing her opponents."
  • "The River card failed to improve his hand, but he decided to bluff and made a large bet."

ROI

Short for “return on investment,” in poker the term is most often used with reference to tournaments as a way to measure and compare players’ profitability.

ROI is calculated as a percentage by dividing one’s profit (the amount one cashes for minus the buy-in) by one’s expense (the buy-in) and multiplying the result by 100. Say a player enters a $100 tournament and cashes for $150. Divide the profit ($50) by the buy-in ($100) to get 0.5, then multiply that by 100 for an ROI of 50%.

Royal Flush

A 'Royal Flush' in poker is the highest possible hand. It consists of the Ace, King, Queen, Jack, and 10, all of the same suit.

What is a Royal Flush in Poker?

In poker, a 'Royal Flush' is the best possible hand a player can have. It consists of the Ace, King, Queen, Jack, and 10, all of the same suit. This hand is unbeatable and is the rarest hand to get in poker games like Texas Hold'em and Omaha.

Understanding a Royal Flush

The chances of being dealt a Royal Flush are extremely low, with odds of 649,739 to 1 in a five-card game. Despite these odds, the excitement and potential winnings from hitting a Royal Flush, especially in high-stakes or tournament play, can be extremely significant.

Playing a Royal Flush

When a player hits a Royal Flush, the strategy is typically to try and maximize the pot. This can involve slow-playing (betting less than the strength of the hand would indicate in order to encourage others to bet more) or aggressive betting to try and build the pot.

Example of a "Royal Flush"

For instance, if a player is dealt the Ace and King of hearts in Texas Hold'em, and the Queen, Jack, and 10 of hearts come on the board, they have a Royal Flush.

  • "He hit a Royal Flush, the best possible hand in poker."
  • "She couldn't believe her luck when she hit a Royal Flush in the high-stakes game."
  • "Despite the odds, he managed to hit a Royal Flush and win the tournament."

Running It Twice

In some cash games, if a player is all in before the river has been dealt, the players involved can agree to "run it twice" which means dealing the remaining streets two times, with half the pot in each instance going to the player making the best hand. (Players can also "run it" more than twice by having the remaining cards dealt more times, if they wish and the game allows it.)

For example, a player with A K goes all in and gets called by another with Q Q and they agree to run it twice. The first board is dealt and comes 2 8 K 9 8 meaning the first player wins with kings, then the second comes 9 3 3 J 7 meaning the second player wins with queens. The two players split the pot, each winning half.
